## What this chapter teaches

We count adjacent transitions inside each training document, normalize one row at a time, and apply additive smoothing. BOS/EOS transitions are included, but no transition crosses a document boundary. Loss, gradients, and evaluation belong to Chapter 7.

{/* chapter-section:worked-example */}
## Predict before counting

For `[0,1,1,2]` and `[0,1,2]`, row 0 sees two `0→1` transitions. Row 1 sees one `1→1` and two `1→2` transitions. With vocabulary size 3 and `α=1`, row 0 becomes `[.200,.600,.200]`; an unseen row becomes uniform.

{/* chapter-section:formula */}
## Formula

$$C_{ij}=\sum_{d\in\mathcal{D}_{tr}}\sum_{t=1}^{|d|-1}\mathbf{1}[z_t=i\land z_{t+1}=j],\quad P(j\mid i)=\frac{C_{ij}+\alpha}{\sum_k C_{ik}+\alpha|V|}$$

Counts are made only from adjacent positions within one training document. Adding `α` to every candidate and dividing by the row total gives a row that sums to one.

{/* chapter-section:symbol-glossary */}
## Symbols

`Cᵢⱼ` is a transition count; `zₜ` is a token; `D_tr` is the training partition; `P(j|i)` is the next-token probability; `α` is positive smoothing; `|V|` is vocabulary size.

{/* chapter-section:history */}
## Historical baseline

Finite-context n-gram models were practical pre-neural language models. Maximum-likelihood counts expose a weakness: an unseen continuation receives probability zero. Our Rust contrast prints that missing evidence beside the smoothed row.

{/* chapter-section:rust-implementation */}
## Rust implementation

`BigramModel::fit` iterates `windows(2)` separately for each document and stores a row-major count table. `count`, `probability`, and `predict` expose the result. Invalid vocabulary sizes, smoothing values, and token IDs are rejected.
