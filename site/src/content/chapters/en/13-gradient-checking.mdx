## Predict one quadratic derivative

Start with the smallest possible gradient check:

```text
q(theta) = theta^2
theta = 3
h = 0.1
```

The two forward probes are `q(2.9)=8.41` and `q(3.1)=9.61`. Their difference
is `1.2`; dividing by `2h=0.2` predicts a numerical derivative of `6`.

That result tests a candidate rather than creating one. The hand derivative of
`theta^2` supplies candidate `6`, which should pass. Candidate `5.5` is also a
perfectly finite number, but it should return a completed comparison with
`pass=false`. A mathematical mismatch is different from an invalid step or a
non-finite objective value.

## Center two probes around one point

The chapter's only display formula is the central difference:

$$
f'(\theta)\approx\frac{f(\theta+h)-f(\theta-h)}{2h}
$$

The two probes sit symmetrically around the same point. For a smooth function,
their leading first-order truncation terms cancel. The approximation still uses
finite `h`, however, and the computer still rounds both function values before
subtracting them. Shrinking `h` therefore helps only until cancellation becomes
the larger source of error.

The implementation requires finite `theta`, positive finite `h`, a finite
`2h`, two finite perturbations that differ from `theta`, and finite function
values. It evaluates the minus side first and the plus side second. A collapsed
probe is rejected rather than reported as a zero derivative.

## Name the derivative-check quantities

| Symbol | Operational meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| $f$ | The deterministic scalar loss-valued function being probed. |
| $\theta$ | The finite scalar parameter or selected tensor coordinate. |
| $h$ | The positive finite perturbation on both sides of the point. |
| $f'(\theta)$ | The derivative at the point approximated by the centered secant slope. |

For analytic candidate `a` and numerical value `n`, Rust chooses
`scale=max(1,abs(a),abs(n))` and records
`scaled_error=abs(a/scale-n/scale)`. The floor at one behaves like absolute
error for small gradients; larger gradients are compared relative to their
magnitude. The candidate passes exactly when scaled error is no greater than
the declared finite nonnegative tolerance.

## From next-word backpropagation to checked Transformer training

Bengio et al.'s neural language model maximizes next-word log-likelihood with an explicit backward/update phase over output, hidden, and learned word-feature parameters. Those propagated derivatives make repeated training updates practical, but the implemented derivative path is not an independent check of itself.

The earlier checkpoint is
[Bengio et al., *A Neural Probabilistic Language Model*](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume3/bengio03a/bengio03a.pdf).
Bengio et al. maximize next-word log-likelihood and publish a backward/update phase that propagates gradients through output units, hidden weights, and learned word-feature vectors.

The Transformer carries gradient-based training into repeated attention and feed-forward layers, using Adam for 100,000 base-model or 300,000 big-model steps. Baydin et al. distinguish finite-difference probes from reverse-mode automatic differentiation: central differences expose local derivative mistakes, while reverse mode efficiently produces a scalar objective's gradient over many parameters.

The later model checkpoint is
[Vaswani et al., *Attention Is All You Need*](https://papers.nips.cc/paper_files/paper/2017/file/3f5ee243547dee91fbd053c1c4a845aa-Paper.pdf).
Vaswani et al. train Transformer base models for 100,000 steps and big models for 300,000 steps, using Adam with an explicit learning-rate schedule.

The numerical-method checkpoint is
[Baydin et al., *Automatic Differentiation in Machine Learning: a Survey*](https://arxiv.org/abs/1502.05767).
Baydin et al. describe centered finite differences, the truncation-versus-round-off step-size trade-off, poor scaling for full numerical gradients, and reverse mode's efficiency for a scalar objective with many parameters.

This chapter uses central differences only as a slow sampled oracle for analytic candidates, including the Chapter 12 indexed mean NLL derivative, before Chapter 14 builds reverse mode. It does not train or run the decoder; its step size, tolerance, coordinate selection, restoration, finite-input, storage, and error-order rules are course-local.

Neither model paper claims to use this checker. Finite differences are not a
later LLM invention, a replacement training algorithm, or decoder runtime work.
Their role here is narrower: give future backward code an independent forward-
loss comparison before its values can update language-model parameters.

## Implement a sampled numerical oracle

Typed errors separate a bad configuration from a failed finite comparison. The
checker names the rejected side and wraps tensor failures with the affected
coordinate. It validates the complete selected coordinate set before the first
objective call, so one late non-finite candidate cannot leave a half-run trace.

For shape `[2,3]`, `N=6` and four samples use flat offsets
`floor(k*(N-1)/(S-1))`: `[0,1,3,5]`. They map to
`[[0,0],[0,1],[1,0],[1,2]]`. No random generator or hidden state is involved.
A one-sample request chooses the middle flat offset. The general sampler uses
an overflow-safe intermediate and the same suffix-product shape semantics as
the cumulative tensor storage.

The tensor checker temporarily writes `theta-h`, evaluates through `&Tensor`,
restores the exact source bits, then repeats for `theta+h`. Restoration occurs
before the result is inspected, including ordinary error paths. A panic inside
the learner-supplied objective is explicitly outside this small reference
guarantee.

## Watch the step size help, then hurt

For `g(theta)=theta^3-2theta` at `theta=1.5`, the analytic derivative is `4.75`.
The Rust scan starts with `h=1`, improves through `1e-1` and `1e-3`, and reaches
its lowest displayed scaled error at `h=1e-5`. `h=1e-8` is already worse even
though it still passes. At `h=1e-12`, rounding has raised the displayed result
to `4.750422277766`, which fails tolerance again. The lesson does not promote
`1e-5` as a universal optimum; it is the trustworthy middle for this fixture.

The strict 23-line Rust trace owns every point, function value, error, phase,
status, sample, and failure. TypeScript checks exact lexemes and ordering but
does not differentiate, scale an error, or select a coordinate. The visual
brackets are explicitly ordinal and not to scale; the semantic table retains
all exact evidence.

## Predict before running Rust

1. Calculate `q(2.9)`, `q(3.1)`, and their central difference at `h=0.1`.
2. Predict the pass states of candidates `6` and `5.5` at tolerance `1e-6`.
3. Select the lowest-error row in this staged scan without calling its step universally optimal.
4. Explain why `h=1e-12` is worse than the larger trusted step.
5. Compute the wrong candidate's scale and scaled error before reading the answer.
6. Derive four flat samples and coordinates for `N=6` and `S=4`.
7. Predict zero-step and collapsed-probe behavior plus tensor restoration on ordinary errors.
8. Misconception check: do four passing coordinates prove every gradient or train or run the decoder?

<details>
  <summary>Check the predictions</summary>

1. `q(2.9)=8.41`, `q(3.1)=9.61`, and `(9.61-8.41)/0.2=6`.
2. Candidate `6` passes. Candidate `5.5` returns a finite comparison with `pass=false`.
3. `h=1e-5` has the lowest displayed scaled error in this fixed scan. Another function, point, or floating type can have a different best region.
4. The two rounded function values become nearly equal, so their subtraction loses useful low bits and rounding dominates.
5. `scale=max(1,5.5,6)=6`; `abs(5.5/6-6/6)=1/12`, far above `1e-6`.
6. `floor(k*5/3)` gives flats `[0,1,3,5]`, hence coordinates `[[0,0],[0,1],[1,0],[1,2]]`.
7. Zero `h` is invalid; `h=1e-20` at point `1` rounds the minus probe back to the point. On every ordinary error, a temporary tensor value is restored before return.
8. No. The samples are evidence only for selected coordinates. Gradcheck neither creates the training gradient nor belongs in decoder inference.

</details>

## Prepare reverse-mode differentiation

The cumulative project can now test a hand-derived candidate for selected vocabulary-logit derivatives against independent forward evaluations of stable indexed mean NLL. Chapter 14 will build reverse-mode scalar derivatives and prove them against this oracle before tensor autodiff or training is trusted.

The next chapter will store scalar computation nodes, apply local derivative
rules in reverse topological order, and accumulate contributions when one value
is reused. Its analytic results will not be trusted merely because the backward
pass runs: the central-difference checker from this chapter becomes the
independent acceptance evidence.
